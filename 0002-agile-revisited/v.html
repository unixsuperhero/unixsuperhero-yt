Breaking Down Stories

  • You can break stories down into smaller stories...
  • But Stories and Tasks are different.
    • Stories are for customers and describe high-level functionality
    • Tasks are for developers

Breaking Down Stories

"Users can securely transfer money into, out of, and between their accounts."
  • Users can login
  • Users can logout
  • Users can deposit money into their account
  • Users can withdraw money from their account
  • Users can transfer money from their account to another account
  • Notice the language
  • Every sentence starts with "Users can..."
  • No mention of DBs, UIs, etc.

Halfway Point

  • Verify that 1/2 of the Story Points are complete (not tasks!)
  • Otherwise, make adjustments
  •  
  • Beware of the Nightmare Scenario
    The iteration ends and 90% of Tasks are finished, but 0 Stories were completed.

After Iteration

  • Calculate velocity from completed Stories
  • Developers calculate their budget from completed Tasks
  • Present a demo to the customer
  • Receive feedback in the form of more User Stories

The Difference

  • No Tasks, Task Points, Dev Budgets
  • Devs don't sign-up for anything before the iteration starts

No Tasks

  • Tasks are created as "smaller stories"
  • Those stories are then distributed across iterations
  • Too many "stories" are added to an iteration
  • The app is always in an unfinished state
  • Partial stories are being delivered
  • No separate levels of abstraction
  • Customers are being exposed to technical details
  • A single dev is responsible for the whole story, instead of just a task. No teamwork.
    • No shared goals or objectives
    • Conway's Law - every dev is isolated, to try to cover more ground, but negatively affects the cohesion of your app. because no one is on the same page

Not Assigning Tasks Before Iteration

  • The iteration is a free-for-all
  • All prep is thrown out the window - unable to plan ahead because you don't know who is doing what
  • Knowing who is doing what, lets you coordinate and collaborate; increases communication
  • Knowing what you're responsible for over the iteration, lets you give more realistic answers when asked about you confidence in completion by the end of the sprint
  • It also lets you think ahead while you're working on something else. So when you start you have a plan of attack.
